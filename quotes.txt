Koalas are marsupials, not bears.

Joeys (baby koalas) are carried in the mother’s pouch.

Joeys (baby koalas) stay in the mother’s pouch for about six months.

Koalas eat mostly eucalyptus leaves.

Koalas can eat up to a kilogram of leaves per day.

Koalas have a strong sense of smell to select the best leaves.

Koalas sleep 18–22 hours a day to save energy.

Koalas are mostly active at night (nocturnal).

Koalas have sharp claws and strong limbs to climb trees.

Koalas have thick, soft fur to protect against heat and rain.

Each koala has a unique nose pattern, like human fingerprints.

Koalas communicate using deep grunts and bellows.

Male koalas have a scent gland on the chest to mark trees.

Koalas live in eucalyptus forests in eastern and southern Australia.

Koalas get most of their water from eucalyptus leaves.

Joeys start eating eucalyptus leaves at about 4–6 months old.

Koalas can live up to 13–18 years in the wild.

Koala populations are threatened by habitat loss due to deforestation.

Koalas lose homes and food sources when bushfires occur.

Koalas often get sick from chlamydia, a common disease.

Koalas are mostly solitary but share overlapping home ranges.

Koalas have a slow metabolism because eucalyptus leaves are low in nutrients.

Koalas have two thumbs on their front paws to help grip branches.

Koalas rely more on hearing and smell than vision.

Koalas can survive on a limited diet that is toxic to most animals.

Koalas can sleep sitting upright in trees.

Mother koalas clean their babies and keep them safe in the pouch.

Koalas sometimes fall from trees when startled or scared.

Koalas are a symbol of wildlife conservation in Australia.

Koalas have been on Earth for millions of years and are part of Australia’s unique fauna.
